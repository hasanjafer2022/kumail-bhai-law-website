CRIMINAL LITIGATION

The Indian Constitution provides the framework for criminal litigation in India.

The key provisions related to criminal litigation are primarily found in the following parts of the Constitution:

1. Fundamental Rights: Part III of the Indian Constitution contains the Fundamental Rights that protect the basic rights and liberties of citizens.
Several rights are relevant to criminal litigation, including:

a. Right to Equality:Article 14 ensures equality before the law and equal protection of the law.

b. Right to Life and Personal Liberty: Article 21 guarantees the right to life and personal liberty, which includes safeguards
against arbitrary arrest and detention.

c. Right against Self-Incrimination:Article 20(3) prohibits self-incrimination, ensuring that no person accused of an offense
shall be compelled to be a witness against themselves.

d. Right to Legal Representation: Article 22 provides safeguards regarding arrest and detention,
including the right to be defended by a legal practitioner of one's choice.

2. Criminal Procedure: The procedural aspects of criminal litigation are governed by the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC).
The CrPC lays down the procedures for investigation, arrest, bail,
trial, sentencing, and appeals in criminal cases.

3. Separation of Powers: The Constitution provides for the separation of powers between the legislature, executive, and judiciary.
This ensures an independent judiciary, which plays a crucial role in criminal
litigation by interpreting the law and ensuring fair trials.

4.Judicial Review: The Indian judiciary has the power of judicial review to examine the constitutionality of laws,
including those related to criminal offenses. If a law is found to be unconstitutional,
it can be struck down.

5.Right to Fair Trial: Article 21 of the Constitution guarantees the right to a fair trial.This includes the right to a speedy trial,
the presumption of innocence until proven guilty, the right to present a defense,
the right to cross-examine witnesses, and the right to be heard.

6. Punishment and Sentencing: The Constitution provides guidelines for punishment and sentencing.
It prohibits cruel and unusual punishment under Article 21
and emphasizes the principle of proportionality in sentencing.

It's important to note that the Constitution provides the broad framework for criminal litigation, and the detailed procedures,offenses, and penalties are covered
by various statutes and laws enacted by the Parliament and State legislatures, such as the Indian Penal Code, the Evidence Act,
and specific laws for different offenses (e.g., the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, the Prevention of Corruption Act, etc.).
These statutes work in conjunction with the Constitution to govern criminal litigation in India.
